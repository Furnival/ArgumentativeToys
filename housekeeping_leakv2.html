This pass might sell for $16,700. Every decimal place of housekeeping precision is a security decision.

A modern shared satellite bus needs to report its own health to every tenant: bus voltage, radiator temperatures, and — crucially — the attitude quaternion. A tenant that points its payload at Kaliningrad has just told every other tenant on board that it pointed at Kaliningrad, at 4 Hz, in double-precision floats.

Coarsen the reported attitude and the pointing footprint blurs. Coarsen it enough and the ellipse contains three countries — a fact of no commercial value. Somewhere between those extremes lies the operator's disclosure policy.

Source: OSM coordinates; Maxar/Planet public pricing; BCT star tracker specs. This is a toy, not a simulation — the world map is Equirectangular and the orbit is a great-circle approximation.

Assumptions & math — how the very fictional dollar figures are computed

Physical constants

  • Earth radius R = 6,371 km (mean, IUGG).
  • Orbit altitude h = 550 km — Starlink v1.5 nominal shell, chosen as the modal LEO for shared-bus commercial imagery and comms today (Starlink technology page).
  • Attitude reporting rate 4 Hz — typical bus telemetry cadence for pointing state on modern shared platforms.

Attitude precision — slider range

  • research σ = 10-4° ≈ 0.36 arcsec — better than the Blue Canyon Technologies Nano Star Tracker (~3 arcsec, 3σ), i.e. laboratory-grade.
  • commercial σ = 10-2° ≈ 36 arcsec — representative of what modern smallsat star trackers actually achieve in flight.
  • coarse σ = 100° = 1° — the sun-sensor / magnetometer regime described in NASA's CubeSat 101 guide.
  • redacted σ = 101° = 10° — a deliberately-degraded posture: what an operator might claim they publish to "protect" the customer. The toy exists to show this claim doesn't hold.

Geometry — footprint radius on the ground

slant_range = √((R+h)² + R² − 2(R+h)·R·cos(d/R))
footprint_radius = slant_range × tan(σ)

Here d is the great-circle distance from the sub-satellite point to the target. Overhead (d≈0) the slant range is ~550 km; near the horizon of the pass it grows past 1,500 km, so the same σ paints a bigger ellipse — the ellipse visibly breathes across a 5-minute window.

The drawn ellipse is amplified when the true radius is sub-pixel (small radii are boosted logarithmically so slider changes stay legible). When amplification is active a "true X m · amplified" tag appears below the target.

Intelligence pricing — where the dollars come from

Two public anchors:

  • Commercial optical imagery. LandInfo's published per-km² rate card (Jan 2026) lists fresh 30 cm Maxar imagery at roughly $20–$30 per km² for a single AOI — the modern reference for a commercial optical pass.
  • SIGINT / sensitive-geography premium. Open-source discussions of the commercial SIGINT market (e.g. HawkEye 360 disclosures, NRO commercial RF contract awards) imply a 10×–100× premium over pure optical for a well-characterised, hard-to-reach target set.

The base value of each preset is the pass's worth if the ellipse resolves exactly one named target. Ambiguity divides that value linearly by the number of candidates the ellipse contains:

earned = base_value / max(1, POIs_in_ellipse)

Two things to notice. First, the value cliff is between 0.1° and 1° — that is exactly where a typical smallsat's cheap fallback attitude sensor lives, and it is where the operator's disclosure policy actually matters. Second, pushing from coarse (1°) to "redacted" (10°) barely helps: the local POI density is already saturated, so the extra ambiguity is not paid for. The illusion of protection collapses quickly.

What this toy is not

These are illustrative anchors, not procurement quotes. Real intelligence pricing is bilateral, classified, and non-linear. The value of housekeeping-leak intelligence is bounded above by the imagery / SIGINT prices cited here — no one pays more for a leaked pointing quaternion than for the equivalent primary collection — and the point of the toy is that the leak is priced at $0 today.
The world map is Equirectangular; the orbit is a great-circle approximation with constant altitude. Sub-satellite ground speed is a two-endpoint interpolation, not a Kepler propagation. None of these simplifications change the argument, which depends only on σ × slant → footprint and on the density of named targets.
