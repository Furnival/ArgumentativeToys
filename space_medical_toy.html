The Problem with Waiting

This toy is an argumentative model, not a faithful medical simulator. It simplifies a single question: how do you shape a communications channel when a time-critical medical decision has to cross distance and latency? The case here is space medicine, but the same tradeoffs appear in any remote outpost, from orbital stations to Antarctica. As the outpost moves farther from help, delay becomes its own clinical variable, and a narrow, structured channel can start to outperform a richer, bedside-style exchange. The medium shifts from conversation toward a coded decision tree: less interactive, more predetermined, but able to carry a treatment decision in time to matter. Yet if you use medication while uncertain of the prognosis, you may run out of your limited supply of medication for this patient or for future ones.

Model notes: where this toy lies
  • Vitals decay linearly per turn. Real septic shock decompensates nonlinearly and can crash without warning.
  • Certainty and supplies are unitless 0-100 scores standing in for a much messier diagnostic and logistics picture.
  • Latency numbers use rounded, representative one-way light-time and mission-profile figures, not live ephemeris data.
  • Crew cognitive load is a proxy for real factors like fatigue, stress, and training gaps, not a measured quantity.
  • There are exactly five actions and six turns. Real triage has no fixed clock and far more options.
  • The three preset scenarios are arguments, not predictions. Changing a dropdown after a preset loads breaks the story it was telling.

The channel that can carry a decision before the patient can't use it beats the channel that would have explained it better.
